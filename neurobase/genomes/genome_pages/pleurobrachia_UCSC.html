Additional details

Pleurobrachia bachei is a simple animal that lives, often in swarms, near the surface of the coastal northeast Pacific Ocean. The body of P. bachei is nearly spherical, to about 2 cm diameter, and is soft and jellylike, of glassy transparency. The outside of this spherical body possesses eight evenly-spaced longitudinal ribs, each about 1 mm wide and tapering at each end; the ribs run nearly from pole to pole. Each of the ribs is composed of about 50 small, overlapping, colorless combs, each composed of hundreds of long, compound cilia (Horridge, 1974), which beat together, thus propelling the animal. Normally, the effective stroke of a comb plate is directed towards the aboral end, so that the ctenophore typically swims mouth first (Tamm, 1973). The combs beat sequentially, passing metachronal waves along the ribs in the opposite direction to the effective stroke, and the comb rows are separately controlled so that the animal can swim forward, or backward, and even spin (Tamm and Moss, 1985).

Pleurobrachia bachei is a carnivorous predator, capturing small zooplankton prey on two pinkish, contractile, filamentous tentacles, each of which has numerous fine side branches. The tentacles exit through two small holes on opposite sides of the body closer to the aboral pole and can extend out many cm beyond the animal, or can be contracted entirely into two sheaths inside the body that angle up towards the gut (Hyman, 1940).

The Pleurobrachia bachei draft genome was assembled using a custom approach designed to leverage the individual strengths of three popular de novo assembly packages and strategies: Velvet, SOAPdenovo, and pseudo-454 hybrid assembly with ABySS. 156,146,497bp of genome sequence were assembled from 1,924,202,519bp of 454 data and 97,391,397,588bp of Illumina HiSeq data. 19,525 gene models are currently predicted for this genome, after removing redundant gene models produced using Augustus version 2.5.5. Version 1.1 of the genome was produced by screening and removing contamination from prokaryotic sequences from the original draft genome. For more information on the assembly, see the Moroz Lab Pleurobrachia Genome Project page.
